## 🧭 Overview
The AAA framework is the standard for managing network access. It ensures that users are who they say they are, control what they can do, and keep a detailed record of their actions.



## 🏗️ The Three Pillars of AAA

### 1. 🆔 Authentication
Proving you are who you claim to be. This happens after **Identification** (stating your username).
* **Mechanism:** Verifying credentials (passwords, MFA factors, or digital certificates).
* **Goal:** Confirm the "claim" of identity is true.

### 2. 🔐 Authorization
Determining what you are allowed to do once you are inside the system.
* **Mechanism:** Access control models (like Roles or Groups).
* **Goal:** Restrict access to specific resources (e.g., Shipping vs. Finance data).

### 3. 📑 Accounting
Keeping a detailed log of all activity.
* **Data Tracked:** Login/logout times, data transferred, and resources accessed.
* **Goal:** Create an audit trail for security reviews and troubleshooting.

---

## 🛠️ Practical Example: VPN Access
When logging into a VPN, a **VPN Concentrator** acts as the middleman between you and a central **AAA Server**.

1. **Request:** The client sends credentials to the VPN Concentrator.
2. **Verification:** The Concentrator forwards these to the **AAA Server** (which holds the central database).
3. **Approval:** The AAA Server tells the Concentrator "Yes" or "No."
4. **Access:** If approved, the user is authorized to access internal file servers.

## 💻 Machine Authentication & Certificates
How do we authenticate a computer (like a laptop) that can't type a password?
* **Digital Certificates:** A "company ID" for the hardware.
* **Certificate Authority (CA):** A server responsible for issuing and signing these certificates.
* **Process:** 
   1. The CA signs a unique certificate for the laptop.
   2. The laptop presents this during login.
   3. The network checks the signature against the **Root CA** to verify it is a trusted company device.

## 📈 Scalability: The Authorization Model
Manually assigning permissions to every single user does not scale in large organizations.

### The Problem: Manual Mapping
If 100 people in "Shipping" each need access to 10 different systems, that is **1,000 manual entries**. This is prone to error and impossible to manage.

### The Solution: Abstraction (Groups/Roles)
We place an **Authorization Model** in the middle to separate users from resources.
* **How it works:** 
   1. Create a **Group** (e.g., "Shipping & Receiving").
   2. Assign all 10 resources to that **Group**.
   3. Add all 100 users to that **Group**.
* **Result:** Adding or removing a user's access now takes **one click** instead of ten.
